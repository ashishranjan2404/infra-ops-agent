# SRE-Degrees — Rebuttal-Anticipation Doc

**Purpose.** Anticipate what reviewers (AAAI / NeurIPS Datasets&Benchmarks / ICLR flavor) will
attack, state each attack in its *strongest* form, and give the *best honest* response — not
spin. Every attack is grounded in a real property of the system, not a strawman. Where an attack
is genuinely dangerous, the response says what we'd have to *do*, not that we're already fine.

Grounding sources: `ARCHITECTURE.md`, `rex/scoring.py` (reward
`0.30·diagnosis + 0.25·fix + 0.45·resolved − 0.60·trap`), the curriculum (15 generated +
5 reconstructed-real hard incidents), the inventory (~51 CIDG YAML specs + 19 reconstructed
post-mortem JSON specs), the headline sweep (5 frozen models × 5 hard incidents), and the
training run `opensre-traj` (reward **0.522 → 0.491 over 25 steps** — flat/declining).

---

## Top-line: the two rejections to plan for

- **Highest-probability rejection (mechanical, instantly agreed by other reviewers):** *small N,
  no confidence intervals, no seed counts.* This is the cheapest reject lever and the one most
  likely to actually appear on the review form. It is **survivable** — add incidents, seeds, CIs.
- **Highest-depth rejection (philosophical, but fatal if it lands):* the environment is a
  *caricature* of incident response — single hidden root cause, one correct fix, one trap. If a
  reviewer with on-call experience buys this, **no amount of N fixes it.** This is the one we
  must engage most seriously and cannot fully close in a rebuttal.

Plan the rebuttal budget around these two first; everything else is secondary.

---

## The attacks

### A1 — Small N, no statistics
**Steelman.** The headline table is 5 models × 5 incidents with no seeds, no variance, and no
confidence intervals. A claimed "+0.23 lift" on n=5 incidents is *one incident flipping* from
0.0 to 1.0 (Δ=0.20). With temperature>0 and an LLM judge in the loop, the run-to-run noise is
plausibly larger than the effect. There is no significance test anywhere, so every quantitative
claim in the paper is, strictly, unsupported. I reject on "conclusions not supported by
evidence."
**Honest response.** This is correct and is the limitation we lead with. The mitigation is
mechanical, not conceptual: (1) expand the hard tier from 5 to ≥30 incidents (the spec corpus
already has 19 reconstructed real outages + room to generate more); (2) run k≥5 seeds per
(model, incident) and report mean ± 95% CI via bootstrap; (3) report per-incident reward, not
just the aggregate, so a reviewer sees the spread. We do *not* claim significance until those CIs
exist. The qualitative claim that survives even at n=5 is structural: every model converges to
the *same* escalate-the-unsolvable behavior, which is a behavioral observation, not a mean.
**Probability.** High
**Depth.** Serious
**Closing evidence.** A table with n≥30 incidents, ≥5 seeds, bootstrap 95% CIs, and a paired
test (REx vs baseline within model). That fully neutralizes A1.

### A2 — Construct validity: this isn't incident response
**Steelman.** Real incidents are not "one hidden root cause + one correct fix + one trap." They
are multi-causal, ambiguous, and evolve while you respond; the "trap" depends on blast radius you
cannot know in the moment, and the "correct fix" is contested even in the post-mortem. By
encoding a single ground-truth cause and a single penalized trap, the environment rewards a
puzzle-solving behavior that does not transfer to the messy, partially-observable reality of
on-call. The benchmark measures *the authors' model of incidents*, not incident response.
**Honest response.** We do not claim a digital twin; we claim a *controlled probe* for one
specific, real, and underexplored failure mode — the *misleading cascade* where the loudest
alert points at a downstream victim and the naive fix worsens the outage. That mode is real (we
reconstruct it from public post-mortems: AWS DynamoDB DNS, Cloudflare WAF, CrowdStrike CF291) and
is exactly where naive automation is dangerous. We concede the scope: single-cause, single-trap
is a *simplification*, and we report it as a limitation, framing results as evidence about *this
trap structure*, not about end-to-end on-call competence. We add multi-cause/ambiguous-fix
incidents to the roadmap rather than claiming we cover them.
**Probability.** Medium
**Depth.** Fatal-if-true
**Closing evidence.** A subset of incidents with *multiple admissible fixes* and graded
(not binary) trap penalties, plus a human-SRE agreement study showing our ground-truth labels
match practitioner consensus. Partial agreement (not full closure) is the honest target.

### A3 — Synthetic / reconstructed data may leak the answer
**Steelman.** The training and most eval data are generated by the authors' own DSL, and the
"real" incidents are *reconstructions* of public post-mortems. In both cases the same authors
who wrote the hidden root cause also wrote the reward that checks for it — the environment can
encode the very answer it then rewards, so a model that learns the generator's regularities looks
competent without learning incident response. Reconstruction from a post-mortem that *already
states the root cause* is answer-leakage by construction.
**Honest response.** The sharpest honest version is "reconstruction fidelity," and we accept it.
Two real defenses: (1) the cascade is *emergent*, not scripted — `sim/engine.py` runs one
`propagate()` kernel over a typed dependency graph, and the live Tier-B GKE mesh produces cascades
physically over real HTTP, so the *symptom→victim* mapping is derived, not authored; the author
writes topology, not the alert pattern. (2) The frozen models never see the spec; they see only
the observable alert state, so they cannot read the leaked label. We concede that *we* know the
answer when we write the reward — that is true of every labeled benchmark — and we mitigate with a
held-out set of incidents authored by a *different* person and with blind human relabeling of a
sample. We do not claim zero leakage; we claim the *agent's* input is leakage-free.
**Probability.** High
**Depth.** Serious
**Closing evidence.** A held-out incident set authored/labeled by independent annotators with
inter-annotator agreement, plus an ablation showing models don't exploit generator-specific cues
(e.g. performance is flat under spec-paraphrase / topology-shuffle).

### A4 — The RL ("Degrees") doesn't actually learn; training is flat
**Steelman.** The project is named for degrees of an *RL* curriculum, but the one fine-tuning run
in the repo goes **0.522 → 0.491 over 25 steps** — reward *declined*. So the actual RL
contribution does not work. REx is inference-time tree search over a *frozen* model, i.e. a
scaffold, not learning. Calling this an RL result is unearned; you have an environment and a
prompting loop.
**Honest response.** We concede this directly and rescope. The contribution we stand behind is:
**(environment + root-cause-aware reward + inference-time refinement, REx)** — and the central
empirical claim (REx lifts every frozen model and compresses the cross-provider spread) does not
depend on fine-tuning at all. The flat RFT run is reported as an *honest negative result* with a
diagnosis (`opensre-traj/hud_env_v2.py`/`train_rft_v2.py` note the original reward was flat and
analyze why), and as motivation for the reward redesign — not as a success. We will retitle/reframe
so "RL" is not overclaimed: the environment is *RL-ready* and produces within-group reward spread
suitable for RFT, and getting RFT to climb is stated as open work.
**Probability.** High
**Depth.** Serious
**Closing evidence.** Either (a) an RFT run on the redesigned reward that beats its baseline with
CIs, or (b) a clean reframing that presents the environment + REx as the contribution and the flat
RFT as a documented negative result. (b) is shippable now; (a) is the stronger close.

### A5 — The reward is reward-hackable / the penalties are tuned to look good
**Steelman.** A weighted sum `0.30·diag + 0.25·fix + 0.45·resolved − 0.60·trap` with hand-picked
coefficients is exactly the kind of reward that gets gamed: a model can chase the 0.45 "resolved"
term by thrashing remediations until the metric recovers, and the specific weights (why 0.60 for
the trap and not 0.5?) look reverse-engineered to make the headline numbers land. Show me the
reward isn't just tuned to produce a clean 0.45/1.0/−0.60 separation.
**Honest response.** The design intent is *anti*-hacking and is structural, not just weights:
`resolved` alone caps at 0.45, so a model that restarts/scales until the metric recovers but
*misdiagnoses or trips the trap* scores 0.0 (the −0.60 trap term zeroes it after clamp). The trap
is the dominant signal precisely to defeat the "thrash until green" exploit. We concede the
coefficients are chosen, not derived, and the honest mitigation is a sensitivity analysis: show
the *ranking* of behaviors (clean win > looks-resolved > trap) is invariant across a range of
weight settings, so conclusions don't hinge on 0.60 vs 0.50. We also report the deterministic-judge
variant (`rex/scoring.py`) so the separation isn't an artifact of one LLM judge's mood.
**Probability.** High
**Depth.** Serious
**Closing evidence.** A weight-sensitivity sweep showing behavior ranking is stable, plus an
adversarial-policy probe (a bot that maximizes `resolved` while ignoring diagnosis) that the
reward correctly scores ≈0.

### A6 — 0.86 is a fixed point, not a result
**Steelman.** All five models converge to *exactly* 0.86 with REx. That's because
0.86 = (4×1.0 + 0.30)/5 — solve the 4 solvable incidents perfectly and get the fixed escalation
partial-credit (0.30) on the 5th. So REx isn't "lifting" anything; it's walking every model to the
environment's arithmetic ceiling. The identical convergence is a tell that the metric is saturated
and the search is trivial, not that the models got better at incident response.
**Honest response.** We state this ugly reading first and then defend it, because a competent
reviewer derives the arithmetic unprompted. The defense: reaching that fixed point is exactly the
*correct* behavior we want — solve what's solvable, and **escalate** the one incident with no safe
fix (`singleton_node_notready`) instead of flailing. Zero-shot, models do *not* reach it: baselines
range 0.63–0.81 and many incidents score 0.0 because models trip the trap or fake a fix. The
result is "REx makes every model both solve-and-know-when-to-stop," which is the safety property,
not saturation. We concede 0.86 is a ceiling, and the fix is to *raise the ceiling*: add incidents
with no clean fixed point (graded partial credit, multiple solvable-but-costly fixes) so the metric
has headroom and convergence is no longer suspicious.
**Probability.** Medium
**Depth.** Serious
**Closing evidence.** A harder tier where the max achievable reward is not a single shared
constant, on which models *do not* all converge to the same number — separating "REx helps" from
"everyone hits the ceiling."

### A7 — Single domain; no evidence this generalizes
**Steelman.** Everything is one domain — Kubernetes/microservice incident response — and a narrow
slice of it (cascading dependency outages). There is no evidence the REx-lifts-frozen-models effect
is anything but a property of this one toy environment. A benchmark/method paper needs to show the
phenomenon isn't domain-specific; otherwise it's a single-environment anecdote.
**Honest response.** We concede single-domain and do not claim cross-domain generality. The honest
framing positions the contribution as a *domain-grounded* environment + reward for misleading
cascades, with a *method* (root-cause-aware reward + REx) whose *mechanism* — penalize the trap,
reward the mechanism not the symptom — is domain-transferable in principle. We will not assert that
without evidence. The cheapest generality evidence we can actually produce: show the same
reward-shape and REx loop on a *second* failure family within ops (e.g. data-pipeline / DB
metastability, which the spec corpus already touches — Slack/Consul cache-DB metastable) and report
whether the lift replicates. That is an honest "one more domain," not "general."
**Probability.** Medium
**Depth.** Manageable
**Closing evidence.** Replication of the REx lift on at least one structurally different failure
family, reported even if the effect is weaker.

### A8 — The judge is circular (LLM grading LLMs)
**Steelman.** `diagnosis_correct` is an LLM-judge on "the mechanism." So the grader is itself a
frozen LLM, plausibly of the same family as some graded policies. That's circular: the benchmark's
ground truth is another model's opinion, which can drift, can be gamed by stylistic mimicry, and
biases toward outputs that *sound* like the judge. The whole "anti-reward-hacking" claim collapses
if the anti-hacking check is itself a hackable model.
**Honest response.** Real concern, partially mitigated already. The judge grades against a
*written ground-truth mechanism* from the spec, not free-form preference, which bounds drift; and
`rex/scoring.py` carries a **deterministic** judge variant so the headline separation can be
reproduced without any LLM in the grading loop. We concede that for nuanced diagnoses the LLM judge
is load-bearing, and the honest mitigations are: (1) report agreement between the LLM judge and the
deterministic judge (and human spot-checks); (2) use a judge from a *different* provider than the
policy under test to break same-family bias; (3) publish the judge prompt and ground-truth labels
for audit.
**Probability.** Medium
**Depth.** Serious
**Closing evidence.** LLM-judge ↔ deterministic-judge ↔ human agreement numbers (e.g. Cohen's κ),
plus cross-provider judge robustness showing the ranking is judge-invariant.

### A9 — Not reproducible: gateway models aren't pinned
**Steelman.** The headline table includes gpt-5.5, gemini-3.1-pro, deepseek-v4-pro routed through
the HUD inference gateway. Those endpoints are not version-pinned; "gpt-5.5" today is not the same
weights in six months. The table is therefore not reproducible, and a benchmark whose numbers can't
be reproduced is not a benchmark.
**Honest response.** Valid and standard for any paper benchmarking hosted frontier models. Mitigations
we will adopt: (1) pin exact model identifiers/snapshot dates and record them in the artifact; (2)
log the full request/response (already captured as HUD traces per run, `rex/hud_frontier.py`), so
the *trajectories* are reproducible even if the endpoint drifts; (3) include at least one fully
open, self-hostable model (the curriculum already sweeps open weights) so the core effect is
reproducible without any gateway. We concede hosted-model numbers are a snapshot and label them as
such with capture dates.
**Probability.** Medium
**Depth.** Manageable
**Closing evidence.** Pinned model IDs + capture dates, archived trajectories, and a replication of
the lift on an open-weights model anyone can run.

### A10 — REx is just known inference-time search rebranded
**Steelman.** "Thompson-sampling tree over candidate refinements with harness feedback" is
best-of-N / tree-of-thought / self-refine with a bandit on top — all published. There's no novel
method here; you've applied an existing search loop to a new environment and reported that more
compute helps, which is unsurprising.
**Honest response.** We do not claim REx invents inference-time search; we claim the *combination*
that matters for safety: search guided by a **root-cause-aware reward with a trap penalty and an
escalation/safety gate**, such that the loop's fixed point is "solve what's safe, escalate what
isn't" rather than "maximize resolved." The novel, checkable claim is empirical: with this reward,
search *compresses the cross-provider capability spread* (weakest baseline gains most) and *induces
correct escalation* on the unsolvable incident — a behavior generic best-of-N does not target. We
position the contribution as the environment+reward (the substrate) plus this evidence, not as a new
optimizer.
**Probability.** Medium
**Depth.** Manageable
**Closing evidence.** An ablation isolating the reward's contribution: REx with the trap/escalation
reward vs REx with a plain "resolved" reward, showing the spread-compression and safe-escalation are
properties of the *reward*, not the search.

## Concession ledger (admit these up front)

1. **N is small and currently lacks CIs/seeds.** Stated as the first limitation; fix is mechanical.
2. **The one RFT run is flat (0.522 → 0.491).** Reported as an honest negative result; the
   contribution is rescoped to environment + reward + REx, not fine-tuning.
3. **The environment is a deliberate simplification** (single root cause / single trap) — a
   controlled probe of misleading cascades, not a digital twin of on-call.
4. **Hosted-model numbers are a dated snapshot;** reproducibility rests on archived trajectories +
   an open-weights replication, not on gateway stability.

## What would actually sink the paper

- **If construct validity (A2) lands and we have no human-SRE agreement evidence** — a reviewer who
  shows our ground-truth fixes disagree with practitioner consensus invalidates the reward itself,
  and N cannot save it. This is the single existential risk; prioritize even a small human-agreement
  study.
- **If A3 + A8 compound** — i.e. the data leaks the answer *and* the grader is a same-family LLM —
  the result could be "models agreeing with the authors' model," which is unfalsifiable. The
  independent-annotator held-out set + cross-provider/deterministic judge agreement is the joint
  defense; without it, the result is not credibly about the world.
- Everything else (A1 small-N, A4 flat-RFT, A6 fixed-point, A9 pinning) is *embarrassing but
  survivable* with honest scoping and the listed mechanical fixes.
