## Limitations

We state the limitations of this work candidly. Several of them are structural
(synthetic data, a single domain) and several emerged from our own internal
red-teaming (a gameable reward sub-score). Where a limitation scopes a result
rather than nullifying it, we say so; where evidence is absent rather than merely
weak, we say that too.

### L1 — Synthetic incident data

Every incident in our evaluation is **fabricated**, not drawn from production
on-call. The headline evaluation set is 42 incidents (10 original hand-authored +
32 procedurally generated by `experiments/build_incidents.py`); the on-disk
generator directory (`scenarios/cidg/generated/`) currently holds 51 YAML specs
after later additions. Each spec encodes an author-defined `topology`,
`root_cause`, `fault`, `trap_actions`, and `canonical_fix`. This means the
incidents reflect *our model* of what an SRE failure looks like — clean causal
chains, a single planted root cause, and a deliberately misleading "loudest alert."
Real incidents have missing telemetry, multiple plausible causes, partial
observability, and noisy ground truth. We have not validated on a single
production incident, so external validity of the absolute pass@k numbers is
unestablished.

### L2 — Oracle circularity (the evaluator shares authorship with the data)

Distinct from L1 (the *incidents* are synthetic), the *evaluator* is co-authored
with them: the same pipeline that plants a trap also defines the deterministic
judge (`rex/scoring.py`) that rewards avoiding it. Reward is
`0.30·diagnosis + 0.25·fix + 0.45·resolved`, and the diagnosis term is graded by
a keyword-set classifier whose "gold mechanism" and "red-herring" keywords are
author-supplied. Consequently every score — including the headline lifts — is
**relative to an author-defined oracle**. Our own probe (D13) makes this explicit:
fool-rate is measured against that oracle, not against an independent notion of
correctness. We do not claim oracle-defined metrics are worthless (SWE-bench,
AIOpsLab, and SREGym share this property); we claim only that confirmation
requires *blind, externally-authored* incidents that we do not yet have.

### L3 — Preliminary reinforcement fine-tuning

The RFT results are directional, not statistically established. The original run
(v1, 4 tasks, 25 steps) **declined**: reward 0.522 → 0.491 (Δ −0.031). The
corrected run (v2, 10 tasks, 15 steps) improved: 0.504 → 0.541 (Δ +0.037)
(`experiments/FINAL_SUMMARY.md`, P4). That improvement is small, spans only 15
optimization steps, uses 10 training tasks, and has no held-out learning curve
reported; the movement is plausibly within the seed-to-seed band. Within-group
reward spread (~0.18) is healthy enough to carry a GRPO gradient, but we do not
claim the policy *learned to do SRE better* — only that the corrected reward and
curriculum produced a positive trend where the prior setup produced a negative
one. A longer run with held-out evaluation and multiple seeds is required before
any transfer or capability claim rests on RFT.

### L4 — Reward-function fragility / reward hacking

Our internal red-team (D13) **broke the diagnosis sub-score**. Across 42 scenarios
× 7 attacks (294 probes) it found 5 working exploit classes against the
deterministic judge: negating the gold ("this is NOT a memory leak") at 100%
fool-rate (because "not" is a stopword), a single gold token with no mechanism
(100%), the right mechanism on the wrong component (100%, no component binding),
homoglyph evasion of the herring penalty (85.7%), and — most dangerous because a
policy could plausibly drift into it — a **hedge that names the gold cause and all
red herrings at once, at 92.9% fool-rate** (`ralph_outputs/D13/SUMMARY.md`). This
is a real, measured fragility, not a hypothetical. It is *scoped* by the reward
weighting — the diagnosis term is at most 0.30 of total reward, so a hedge plus a
genuinely correct fix reaches 0.55, not 1.0 — but we explicitly do **not** offer
"we did not observe a trained policy exploiting it" as reassurance: our RFT runs
were too short (15 steps) to have looked. Whether a longer-trained policy drifts
into the hedge attack is an open question, and the documented mitigations
(negation detection, single-mechanism commitment penalty, component binding,
NFKC/ASCII normalization) are designed, not yet built into the scored harness.

### L5 — Single domain, and unsubstantiated cross-domain transfer

All incidents are in **one domain**: Kubernetes / cloud-infrastructure SRE. We
make no claim outside it. More seriously, our cross-domain transfer claim
(Claim 2: FIREBALL D&D trajectory data improving cascade-incident performance) is
currently **unsubstantiated in-repo, not merely weak**. The supporting GRPO run
was never pushed, and the FIREBALL training corpus is absent from the repository;
P7 is marked **BLOCKED** (`experiments/FINAL_SUMMARY.md`, P7). The structural
argument (FIREBALL's `state_before → action → state_after` shape mirrors an
incident timeline) is suggestive but has no reproducible evidence behind it here.
We therefore report Claim 2 as a hypothesis, not a result.

### L6 — Statistical power, single-model evaluation, and reproducibility gaps

Several headline comparisons are underpowered. In the 5-condition ablation, the
four non-REx conditions are statistically indistinguishable — zero-shot 0.242,
best-of-n 0.235, retry-realistic 0.230, REx-no-oracle 0.250 (a 0.02 band), so the
only separable signal is REx-*with-SME* at 0.687 (`experiments/CLAIMS_EVIDENCE.md`;
`experiments/FINAL_SUMMARY.md`). The intended full ablation (42 incidents × 5
conditions × 5 seeds = 750 episodes) was **only partially completed** — the run
was repeatedly killed on HUD API latency and only the zero-shot/simple cell was
captured (FINAL_SUMMARY, P3). Multiple diagrams rest on a single model (Haiku), so
cross-model robustness and confidence intervals on the headline lift are missing.
Finally, the SME-feedback signal that drives the strongest training claim is
**semi-synthetic**: we authored the corrections the system learns from
(CLAIMS_EVIDENCE, Claim 3).

### Scope — what this does not invalidate

These limitations bound *external validity and statistical strength*; they do not
erase the two load-bearing positive results, which are real within the defined
oracle and reproducible in-repo:

- **Harness synthesis** (Claim 1): a Thompson-sampling-synthesized 3-rule safety
  harness raised held-out verifier accuracy from 66.7% (empty) to 89.7%,
  approaching the 94.9% hand-written ceiling while cutting false-allows from 100%
  to 30.8% (`experiments/tables/table3_harness_synthesis.md`). This result does not
  depend on RFT, cross-domain data, or the gameable diagnosis term.
- **REx with SME feedback** (Claim 3): a 0.242 → 0.687 mean-reward lift (2.8×
  zero-shot) on hard incidents (`rex/runs/ablation.json`).

The honest reading of this paper is: a *method* (synthesized harness + SME-feedback
REx loop) shows a clear, reproducible signal on a synthetic single-domain
benchmark with a partially gameable evaluator, while the RFT and cross-domain
transfer stories remain preliminary or unsubstantiated.
