# F2 — 02 Grill (Ralph Loop, 5 personas × 3 rounds)

Personas: **Senior ML Researcher (SMR)**, **Principal SRE (PSRE)**,
**AAAI Reviewer (REV)**, **RL Engineer (RLE)**, **DevOps Lead (DEV)**.

## Round 1 — initial take

**SMR:** A Limitations section is only worth writing if it pre-empts the obvious
attacks. The big three here are screaming: the incidents are synthetic (51 YAMLs
generated by `build_incidents.py`), everything is one domain (Kubernetes/cloud
SRE), and the RFT is preliminary (+3.7% over 15 steps is barely above noise). Lead
with those.

**PSRE:** From an operations standpoint the killer caveat is that none of this
touched a real on-call incident. Synthetic incidents encode the author's *own*
mental model of what a cascade looks like. Real incidents are messier, have
missing telemetry, and the "loudest alert ≠ root cause" trap is hand-authored.
The transfer to production is unproven and the section must say so bluntly.

**REV:** I'll reject-or-major-revise this if the Limitations section is performative.
Two things I look for: (1) does the reward function actually measure what they
claim, and (2) is the effect statistically real. D13 shows the deterministic judge
is gameable — 5 working exploit classes, hedge attack 92.9% fool-rate. If your own
internal red-team broke your reward and the paper doesn't disclose it, that's a
desk reject. Also the ablation's four non-REx conditions are 0.23–0.25, statistically
indistinguishable.

**RLE:** The RFT story is the weakest. v1 actually *declined* (0.522→0.491). v2
"improves" to +0.037 over only 15 steps with N=10 tasks and no held-out eval curve.
Within-group std ~0.18 is fine for gradients but the absolute movement is inside the
seed-to-seed band. Calling it "learning" is generous. The honest framing is
"preliminary, directionally positive, not statistically established."

**DEV:** Reproducibility is a limitation too. P3 ablation is *partial* — the full
750-episode run was killed on API latency. P7 Fireball is *blocked* — data never
pushed. A reader can't reproduce the headline cross-domain claim. That belongs in
Limitations, not buried in an appendix.

## Round 2 — react to another persona BY NAME (genuine disagreement)

**RLE → REV:** I disagree with REV's framing that the reward hack is a *desk-reject-level*
secret. It's a strength if disclosed correctly. The D13 hack caps at 0.30 of total
reward, and reachability under an actual RL policy is *unproven* — only the hedge
attack is something a policy plausibly drifts into. If we write "our reward is
perfectly gameable" we overstate it. The limitation is "the diagnosis sub-score is
gameable in principle; we have not observed a trained policy exploiting it." Precision
matters or we hand reviewers a worse story than the truth.

**REV → RLE:** Pushback. "Not yet observed" is exactly the sentence that gets a paper
dinged later. You ran 15 RFT steps — of course you didn't observe reward hacking; you
barely trained. Absence of evidence here is evidence of *not having looked*. I'll
accept the 0.30-cap nuance, but the section must NOT lean on "we didn't see it" as
reassurance.

**PSRE → SMR:** I think SMR under-weights the single-domain problem by lumping it with
synthetic data. They're different failures. Synthetic-but-multi-domain would still be
more credible than real-but-single-domain. Here we have *both* synthetic *and*
single-domain, and worse, the synthetic generator and the evaluator share authorship —
the same person who wrote the trap wrote the judge that rewards avoiding the trap. That
circularity is the deepest limitation and deserves its own bullet, not a sub-clause.

**SMR → PSRE:** Partly disagree. The author-circularity critique is real but it proves
*too much* — by that logic every benchmark with authored ground truth (SWE-bench,
AIOpsLab) is invalid. The honest framing isn't "circular therefore worthless," it's
"oracle-defined metrics, so fool-rate is relative to an author oracle (D13 says exactly
this) and external/blind incidents are needed to confirm." Keep it scoped.

**DEV → RLE:** I disagree that RFT is merely "the weakest part." It's a *blocking*
limitation for Claim 2 (cross-domain transfer) because the supporting Fireball run was
never pushed (P7 BLOCKED). So Claim 2 currently has zero reproducible evidence in-repo.
That's not "weak," that's "unsubstantiated." The Limitations section must distinguish
weak-but-present evidence from absent evidence.

## Round 3 — synthesis

Points of agreement after disagreement:
1. **Three headline limitations** (synthetic, single-domain, preliminary-RFT) — keep,
   but split synthetic-data from author-circularity (PSRE won that) and keep circularity
   *scoped* to oracle-relative metrics (SMR's correction).
2. **Reward hacking (D13)** must be disclosed, with REV's discipline: do NOT use "we
   didn't observe it" as comfort; DO state the 0.30 cap and unproven reachability as
   *scope*, not exoneration (RLE+REV synthesis).
3. **Claim-level honesty**: separate weak-but-present evidence (Claim 1 harness, Claim 3
   REx-with-SME) from unsubstantiated/blocked (Claim 2 Fireball, full ablation) — DEV's
   point.
4. **Statistical power**: single-model on several diagrams, four indistinguishable
   ablation conditions, no CIs on the headline lift — REV+RLE.
5. **Reproducibility gaps**: partial ablation (API latency), blocked Fireball — DEV.

These five themes become the section's backbone.
